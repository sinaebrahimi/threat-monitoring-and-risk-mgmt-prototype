\begin{abstract}
Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT) collection and analysis are central to proactive cyber threat monitoring, yet the manual curation of news and advisory feeds cannot scale. This report presents a prototype that uses Artificial Intelligence (AI) to support an OSINT pipeline. The pipeline ingests publicly available news articles from the Guardian Open Platform Application Programming Interface (API), applies multi-stage Natural Language Processing (NLP) to classify threats and extract key entities, and computes a composite risk score to prioritise intelligence items. The system combines a keyword-based threat taxonomy with a zero-shot Natural Language Inference (NLI) classifier and Valence Aware Dictionary and Sentiment Reasoner (VADER) sentiment analysis, producing ranked threat reports without requiring labelled training data. Across a six-month corpus of 1,228 unique articles, the pipeline produces interpretable per-category intelligence briefs, achieves approximately 75\% agreement between keyword and NLI classification on the top 50 articles, and reports a mean risk score of 5.09 out of 10, with 63 Critical and 290 High severity items. The results also highlight the main constraints of the prototype: single-source coverage, English-only processing, and hand-tuned scoring weights.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Open-Source Intelligence, threat intelligence, Natural Language Processing, Named Entity Recognition, zero-shot classification, risk scoring, cyber security, Guardian Open Platform.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% 

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} volume of publicly available threat-relevant information has grown, spanning news outlets, government advisories, social media, and technical vulnerability databases~\cite{comst23sun,Yadav2023}. Analysts monitoring these channels face a severe bottleneck: filtering signal from noise in near-real time is cognitively demanding and error-prone~\cite{zhu2018threat}. Artificial Intelligence (AI) methods, particularly Natural Language Processing (NLP), can support collection, triage, and risk scoring while preserving the interpretability required by operational security teams.

This report presents a prototype that combines standard NLP and classification methods within an Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT) pipeline. The system uses a single open news source, applies several processing stages (cleaning, Named Entity Recognition (NER), classification, and scoring), and ranks the highest-risk articles for analyst review. The emphasis is on design choices and technical trade-offs rather than production hardening. To support reproducibility, the accompanying public GitHub repository includes the notebook, source code, and generated artefacts: \url{https://github.com/sinaebrahimi/threat-monitoring-and-risk-mgmt-prototype}.

\subsection{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
  \item A complete OSINT ingestion and enrichment pipeline that uses freely available Application Programming Interface (API) access and CPU-only models, validated on 1,228 deduplicated articles spanning six months.
  \item A two-stage threat classifier that combines fast keyword rules with zero-shot Natural Language Inference (NLI) and requires no labelled training data.
  \item A composite and interpretable risk-scoring formula that integrates keyword density, sentiment, recency, and entity richness across five threat categories.
  \item A critical discussion of limitations, ethical considerations, and deployment steps for multi-source and analyst-in-the-loop extensions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% 

Early OSINT systems relied on keyword matching and Really Simple Syndication (RSS) aggregation~\cite{Schaurer2010}. Recent NER methods based on Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT)~\cite{devlin2019bert} have improved entity extraction from unstructured text. Platforms such as \emph{Open Cyber Threat Intelligence (OpenCTI)}~\cite{opencti2021} and \emph{Malware Information Sharing Platform (MISP)}~\cite{misp2016} formalise threat intelligence sharing, but still rely on analysts to populate structured records, preserving a manual bottleneck.

Zero-shot text classification~\cite{yin2019benchmark} uses NLI models to score label hypotheses without task-specific training, making it suitable for rapid triage when labelled security data are scarce. Risk quantification approaches range from Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS)-style ordinal scales~\cite{cvss2019} to learned regression models trained on labelled incident datasets. This report draws on these strands while remaining lightweight: the objective is a prototype that practitioners can inspect and tune, rather than a black-box model optimised only for benchmark accuracy.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}
% 

Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows the five-stage pipeline. Each stage is implemented as an independent Python module, so components can be swapped or upgraded without rewriting the rest of the system. The pipeline uses three main data formats: JavaScript Object Notation (JSON), HyperText Markup Language (HTML), and Comma-Separated Values (CSV).

\textbf{Stage 1 (Ingestion):} A \texttt{GuardianCollector} class queries the Guardian \texttt{/search} endpoint across 15 threat-relevant terms over a six-month date window. Articles are deduplicated by Guardian content ID and persisted as raw JSON, supporting offline re-runs without repeated API calls.

\textbf{Stage 2 (Preprocessing):} Article bodies are returned as HTML; \texttt{BeautifulSoup}, with an \texttt{lxml} backend, strips the markup to plain text. A \textit{search text} field is then constructed by concatenating the title (repeated three times for downstream emphasis), the standfirst, and the body, providing a single string for all NLP stages.

\textbf{Stage 3 (Named Entity Recognition):} spaCy's \texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm}~\cite{spacy2} is applied to the first 5000 characters of each article, extracting Organisation (ORG), Geopolitical Entity (GPE), Person (PERSON), Event (EVENT), and Nationalities or Religious or Political Groups (NORP) spans.

\textbf{Stage 4 (Classification):} A two-stage classifier (Sec.~\ref{subsec:cls}) assigns each article to one of five threat categories: \emph{Cyber}, \emph{Geopolitical}, \emph{Terrorism}, \emph{Disinformation}, and \emph{Critical Infrastructure}.

\textbf{Stage 5 (Risk Scoring):} Four normalised component scores are combined into a composite risk score (Sec.~\ref{sec:risk}), scaled to $[0,10]$ and mapped to four severity labels.

\section{Data Collection}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Guardian Open Platform}

The Guardian Open Platform provides free developer-tier access to article full text, metadata, and taxonomic tags via a Representational State Transfer (REST) API~\cite{guardianapi}. The \texttt{/search} endpoint accepts keyword queries, date ranges, section filters, and a \texttt{show-fields} parameter that controls the returned fields. Full article body text is available at no cost, making this a high-quality and reproducible data source for OSINT prototyping.

\subsection{Corpus Construction}

Table~\ref{tab:queries} lists the 15 threat-oriented query terms. For each term, up to three pages of 50 results (150 articles per term) are retrieved, covering a 180-day period from December 2025 to June 2026. After deduplication using Guardian content IDs, the corpus contains 1,228 unique articles. A 0.25-second inter-request delay ensures compliance with the developer-tier rate limit. Articles with fewer than 50 body characters, typically gallery or video-primary content, are removed during preprocessing and account for under 5\% of total records.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% 

\subsection{Named Entity Recognition}

spaCy's statistical NER pipeline identifies named spans within article text and assigns them to entity types. The \texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm} model (12 MB, CPU-only) achieves an F1 score of approximately 0.85 on OntoNotes~\cite{spacy2,ontonotes2013}. To bound runtime, processing is limited to the first 5000 characters of each article; the title and standfirst, typically the most informationally dense fields, always fall within this window. Entity extraction serves two purposes: (i) it provides structured metadata for the threat report by identifying which organisations and countries are implicated in each item; and (ii) it contributes a risk signal, since articles naming more distinct actors tend to describe more concrete, attributable threats.

\subsection{Threat Classification}
\label{subsec:cls}

Classification proceeds in two stages.

\textbf{Stage 1: Keyword classifier (all 1,228 articles):} For each category $c$, keyword density is
\begin{equation}
  K_c = \frac{\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}_c} \mathrm{count}(k,\,\hat{t})}{|\hat{t}|}
  \label{eq:keyword}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{K}_c$ denotes category keywords, $\hat{t}$ the lowercased search text, and $|\hat{t}|$ the word count. The label is $c^*=\arg\max_c K_c$, with keyword score $S_{\mathrm{kw}} = \min(K_{c^*}/0.05,\;1.0)$ capped at a reference density of 0.05 hits per word.

\textbf{Stage 2: Zero-shot NLI classifier (top-50 by keyword score):} The pipeline uses the NLI cross-encoder model~\cite{reimers2019sentencebert}, \texttt{cross-encoder/nli-MiniLM2-L6-H768}, in zero-shot mode~\cite{yin2019benchmark}. For each article, the model evaluates each category label as a natural-language hypothesis and returns a confidence distribution. To bound runtime, this stage is restricted to the top-50 articles, requiring approximately two seconds per article on a CPU. The highest-confidence label and score are stored with the keyword result, enabling consistency checks and illustrating how learned models can complement rule-based classification.

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis}

Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner (VADER)~\cite{hutto2014vader} produces a compound score $v \in [-1,\,1]$ for each article headline and standfirst. The sentiment-risk component is defined as
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{sent}} = \frac{1 - v}{2}
  \label{eq:sentiment}
\end{equation}
so that highly negative text ($v \approx -1$) yields $S_{\mathrm{sent}} \approx 1$, neutral text maps to $0.5$, and positive text approaches $0$. VADER was chosen over a transformer-based sentiment model because it runs in milliseconds on commodity hardware, requires no additional model download, and produces directly interpretable scores.

\section{Risk Assessment Framework}
\label{sec:risk}
% 

\subsection{Component Scores}

Four normalised component scores, each in $[0,\,1]$, contribute to the composite risk score:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Keyword score} $S_{\mathrm{kw}}$: normalised keyword density (Eq.~\ref{eq:keyword}).
  \item \textbf{Sentiment score} $S_{\mathrm{sent}}$: VADER-derived negativity (Eq.~\ref{eq:sentiment}).
  \item \textbf{Recency score} $S_{\mathrm{rec}}$: exponential decay from publication date with a 30-day half-life,
        \begin{equation}
          S_{\mathrm{rec}} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\ln 2 \cdot \Delta t}{30}\right),
          \label{eq:recency}
        \end{equation}
        where $\Delta t$ is the article age in days.
  \item \textbf{Entity score} $S_{\mathrm{ent}}$: normalised named entity count, $\min(n_e / n_{\max},\;1.0)$, where $n_e$ is the article's entity count and $n_{\max}$ is the corpus maximum.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Composite Risk Score}

The final risk score is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Risk} = \Big(0.35\cdot S_{\mathrm{kw}}
    + 0.25\cdot S_{\mathrm{sent}}
    + 0.20\cdot S_{\mathrm{rec}}
    + 0.20\cdot S_{\mathrm{ent}}\Big)
    \times M_c \times 10,
  \label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}
where $M_c$ is a category-specific severity multiplier: Terrorism ($1.5$), Critical Infrastructure ($1.4$), Cyber ($1.3$), Geopolitical ($1.2$), Disinformation ($1.0$), and Unknown ($0.8$). These multipliers encode domain knowledge about the typical operational impact of each category; they are a deliberate design choice, not learned parameters. The score is clipped to $[0,\,10]$ and mapped to four severity labels (Table~\ref{tab:severity}).

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Severity labels and suggested analyst actions}
\label{tab:severity}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Label} & \textbf{Score range} & \textbf{Suggested action} \\
\midrule
Low      & $[0,\,3)$  & Log; review weekly \\
Medium   & $[3,\,6)$  & Monitor; alert on escalation \\
High     & $[6,\,8)$  & Prioritise; analyst review within 24 h \\
Critical & $[8,\,10]$ & Immediate escalation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Threat Report Output}

The pipeline produces two artefacts. The CSV file, \texttt{threat\_report.csv} (555 KB), lists all articles in descending risk-score order, including category, severity label, key entities, and Uniform Resource Locator (URL). The JSON file, \texttt{threat\_report.json} (832 KB), stores the same records for downstream dashboards or Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) platforms. A structured set of per-category \emph{intelligence briefs} (step 7c in the \texttt{threat\_monitoring.ipynb} notebook) extracts the two highest-scoring articles per threat type and summarises title, score, date, key organisations, and locations.

\subsection{Corpus Overview}

The corpus composition in Fig.~\ref{fig:catdist} provides context for the downstream scoring results. In particular, the prevalence of articles classified as Terrorism ($\sim$35\%) interacts with the category multiplier ($M_c = 1.5$), amplifying scores for high-keyword-density matches in that category.


\subsection{Risk Score Distribution}

The composite score distribution is right-skewed: 60.8\% of articles fall in the Medium range ($[3,\,6)$), with 23.6\% in High, 5.1\% in Critical, and 10.4\% in Low. The corpus mean is 5.09 out of 10. Fig.~\ref{fig:scoredist} shows the histogram. The highest-scoring articles (scores of 10.0) cover the Golders Green terrorist attack and its immediate aftermath in the UK~\cite{dodd2026ukTerrorThreatSevere}. These articles combine high keyword density, strong sentiment negativity, and a short elapsed time since publication ($S_{\mathrm{rec}}$ in \eqref{eq:risk}). This ranking follows directly from the scoring formula.

\subsection{Classifier Comparison}

For the top 50 articles by keyword score, the keyword and NLI classifiers agree approximately 75\% of the time. Disagreements arise most often when an article has a moderate density of cyber-related keywords but the zero-shot model assigns a geopolitical framing (e.g., state-sponsored cyberattack reporting). These cases confirm that the two stages are complementary: the keyword classifier provides fast, fully deterministic triage, while the NLI stage captures semantic context that keyword density alone misses. A known limitation is that articles covering the war in Ukraine are occasionally scored as high-risk Terrorism items because they contain dense attack-related vocabulary; more granular subcategories would reduce this artefact in a production system.

\subsection{Risk versus Sentiment}

Fig.~\ref{fig:scatter} shows the relationship between composite risk score and VADER sentiment negativity across the corpus, with points coloured by threat category. High-risk articles cluster at higher negativity values, but the spread is substantial, confirming that sentiment is a meaningful but non-dominant signal. Variation in recency and keyword density introduces this spread, which is the intended behaviour of the composite formula.

\section{Key Considerations}
\label{sec:considerations}
% 

\subsection{Data Quality and Limitations}

The prototype relies on a single English-language news outlet. The Guardian's editorial decisions determine what is published; emerging threats first reported in non-English sources or specialist technical forums, such as threat intelligence blogs, vulnerability disclosure lists, and dark-web forums, are not captured by the system. A production pipeline should integrate multiple independent sources, including Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) advisories and Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) feeds~\cite{cvss2019}, and apply cross-lingual NLP.

Keyword classifiers are inherently vocabulary-bound: novel terminology, such as a newly coined attack name or a zero-day before public disclosure, will not match until the taxonomy is updated. Zero-shot NLI partially mitigates this issue but remains constrained by its training distribution. The scoring weights are hand-tuned rather than data-driven; without labelled ground truth, there is no objective basis for claiming that they are optimal, and different weighting configurations would yield different rankings.

\subsection{Ethical and Legal Considerations}

All data is retrieved under The Guardian's developer API terms, which permit non-commercial use. The prototype processes no personal data beyond article bylines. However, automated threat labelling at scale can generate false positives that, if acted upon without human review, could harm individuals or organisations. A human-in-the-loop review step is therefore essential before any score-driven operational action is taken. The system should not be used to monitor individuals, and any production deployment should include bias audits to ensure that no group is systematically over-represented in high-severity labels.

\subsection{Scalability and Deployment}

The prototype runs on a standard laptop without GPU acceleration. Production deployment would require three architectural changes: (i) replacing manual notebook execution with a scheduled ingestion pipeline, such as Apache Airflow; (ii) replacing CSV files with a vector database, such as Weaviate or Pinecone, to support semantic search and similarity-based deduplication as the corpus grows; and (iii) replacing the small, general-purpose models with fine-tuned cybersecurity NER and classification models. Table~\ref{tab:scalability} summarises these transitions.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Scalability steps: prototype versus production}
\label{tab:scalability}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Prototype} & \textbf{Production} \\
\midrule

A later version could adopt \emph{agentic AI}, using autonomous agents to monitor multiple sources, escalate newly identified high-risk items, coordinate analyst workflows, and refine scoring parameters from analyst feedback. Such a design would add agent-based reasoning and broaden source coverage across surface-web, deep-web, and other open-source channels.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% 

This report has presented a prototype OSINT threat monitoring system that runs an end-to-end pipeline from API ingestion to ranked threat reports, using only CPU-friendly, freely available NLP tools. Across 1,228 articles, the pipeline achieves a corpus mean risk score of 5.09, identifies Critical items with scores up to 10.0, and produces structured intelligence briefs across five threat categories. The two-stage classifier requires no labelled data, and the composite risk score is fully interpretable. The main limitations are single-source coverage, English-only processing, and hand-tuned weights.

Future work should focus on: (i) integrating CISA advisories and CVE feeds for enriched technical context; (ii) mapping extracted entities to the MITRE Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge (ATT\&CK) framework~\cite{strom2018mitre} for structured threat attribution; (iii) building a knowledge graph of threat actors, tools, and campaigns; (iv) applying active learning with analyst feedback to refine classification and scoring weights; and (v) developing a real-time Streamlit dashboard as the operational interface. Over time, this could evolve into a fully agentic architecture capable of autonomous multi-source OSINT collection and triage.
