% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Open-source intelligence (OSINT) collection and analysis is central to
proactive cyber threat monitoring, yet manual curation of news and advisory
feeds does not scale. This paper presents a prototype AI-driven OSINT pipeline
that ingests publicly available news articles from \emph{The Guardian Open
Platform}, applies multi-stage natural language processing (NLP) to classify
threats and extract key entities, and computes a composite risk score to
prioritise intelligence items. The system combines a keyword-based threat
taxonomy with a zero-shot natural language inference (NLI) classifier and VADER
sentiment analysis, producing ranked threat reports without requiring labelled
training data. Over a six-month corpus of 1,228 unique articles, the pipeline
produces interpretable per-category intelligence briefs, achieves approximately
75\% agreement between keyword and NLI classification on the top-50 articles,
and surfaces a mean risk score of 5.09 out of 10, with 63 Critical and 290 High
severity items. Key limitations, including single-source coverage, English-only
processing, and hand-tuned scoring weights, motivate a clear roadmap toward a
production-grade, agentic threat intelligence system.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
OSINT, threat intelligence, NLP, named entity recognition, zero-shot
classification, risk scoring, cyber security, Guardian API.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} volume of publicly available threat-relevant information
has grown substantially, spanning news outlets, government advisories, social
media, and technical vulnerability databases~\cite{li2023osint}. Security
analysts who monitor these channels manually face a severe bottleneck: filtering
signal from noise in near-real time is cognitively demanding and prone to
error~\cite{zhu2018threat}. AI methods, particularly NLP, offer a principled
path to automating collection, triage, and risk scoring, while preserving the
interpretability that operational security teams require.

This paper describes a prototype designed to show that foundational AI
techniques can be combined into a coherent OSINT pipeline. The system targets
a single high-quality open news source, applies progressively richer NLP
stages (cleaning, NER, classification, scoring), and surfaces the
highest-risk articles as actionable threat intelligence items. The focus is on
sound technical reasoning and clear design decisions, not production hardening.

\subsection*{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
  \item A complete OSINT ingestion and enrichment pipeline using freely
        available APIs and CPU-only models, validated over 1,228 deduplicated
        articles spanning six months.
  \item A two-stage threat classifier combining fast keyword rules with
        zero-shot NLI, requiring no labelled training data.
  \item A composite, interpretable risk-scoring formula integrating keyword
        density, sentiment, recency, and entity richness across five threat
        categories.
  \item A critical discussion of limitations, ethical considerations, and
        a scalability roadmap including a path toward agentic AI architectures.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Early OSINT systems relied on keyword matching and RSS feed
aggregation~\cite{Schaurer2010}. The emergence of deep learning-based NER
built on BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert} substantially improved entity extraction
quality on unstructured text. Platforms such as
\emph{OpenCTI}~\cite{opencti2021} and \emph{MISP}~\cite{misp2016} formalise
threat intelligence sharing but rely on human analysts to populate structured
records, creating exactly the bottleneck that automated pipelines aim to reduce.

Zero-shot text classification~\cite{yin2019benchmark}, which uses NLI models
to score hypotheses without task-specific training, has been applied to
security triage~\cite{brown2023zeroshot}. Risk quantification approaches range
from CVSS-style ordinal scales~\cite{cvss2019} to learned regression models
over labelled incident datasets. The present work draws on all of these strands
while remaining deliberately lightweight: the objective is a deployable prototype
that a practitioner can inspect and tune, not a black-box model with marginally
higher benchmark accuracy.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows the five-stage pipeline. Each stage is implemented
as an independent Python module, enabling components to be swapped or upgraded
without rewriting the rest of the system.

  \node[box=violet, right=of prep]    (ner)   {3.\ NER\\spaCy entities};
  \node[box=orange, right=of ner]     (cls)   {4.\ Classification\\Keyword + ZS-NLI};

\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Five-stage pipeline from API ingestion to ranked threat report.}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Stage 1 (Ingestion).}
A \texttt{GuardianCollector} class queries the Guardian \texttt{/search}
endpoint across 15 threat-relevant terms with a six-month date window.
Articles are deduplicated by canonical ID and persisted as raw JSON, supporting
offline re-runs without repeated API calls.

\textbf{Stage 2 (Preprocessing).}
Article bodies arrive as HTML; BeautifulSoup with an lxml backend strips
markup to plain text. A \textit{search text} field is then constructed by
concatenating the title (repeated three times for downstream emphasis),
the standfirst, and the body, providing a single string for all NLP stages.

\textbf{Stage 3 (Named Entity Recognition).}
SpaCy's \texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm}~\cite{spacy2} is applied to the first
5\,000 characters of each article, extracting organisations (ORG),
geopolitical entities (GPE), persons (PERSON), events (EVENT), and
nationalities (NORP).

\textbf{Stage 4 (Classification).}
A two-stage classifier (Sec.~\ref{sec:ai}) assigns each article to one of
five threat categories: \emph{Cyber}, \emph{Geopolitical}, \emph{Terrorism},
\emph{Disinformation}, and \emph{Critical Infrastructure}.

\textbf{Stage 5 (Risk Scoring).}
Four normalised component scores are combined into a composite risk score
(Sec.~\ref{sec:risk}), scaled to $[0,10]$ and mapped to four severity labels.

\section{Data Collection}
\label{sec:data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Guardian Open Platform}

The Guardian Open Platform provides free developer-tier access to the full
text, metadata, and taxonomic tags of all published articles via a REST
API~\cite{guardianapi}. The \texttt{/search} endpoint accepts keyword queries,
date ranges, section filters, and a \texttt{show-fields} parameter controlling
returned fields. Full article body text is available at no cost, making
this a high-quality and reproducible data source for OSINT prototyping.

\subsection{Corpus Construction}

Table~\ref{tab:queries} lists the 15 threat-oriented query terms. For each
term, up to three pages of 50 results (150 articles per term) are retrieved
covering December 2025 to June 2026. After deduplication by Guardian content
ID, the corpus contains 1,228 unique articles. A 0.25-second inter-request
delay respects the developer-tier rate limit. Articles with fewer than 50
body characters (gallery or video-primary content) are dropped after
preprocessing, accounting for under 5\% of total records.

\textbf{Category} & \textbf{Query terms} \\
\midrule
Cyber & ransomware, data breach, malware, phishing, \\
      & DDoS attack, zero-day vulnerability, supply chain attack \\
Geopolitical & espionage, nation state hacking, cyber warfare \\

\subsection{Dataset Characteristics}

The Terrorism category dominates with 432 articles (35.2\%), followed by Cyber
(244, 19.9\%), Critical Infrastructure (215, 17.5\%), Geopolitical (207, 16.9\%),
Unknown (90, 7.3\%), and Disinformation (40, 3.3\%). This distribution reflects
both the relative volume of Guardian reporting on each topic and the number of
query terms per category, with Cyber benefiting from the larger number of targeted
search terms. The most represented sections are \texttt{world}, \texttt{technology},
and \texttt{uk-news}, with a mean article word count of approximately 650.

\section{AI and Analytical Methods}
\label{sec:ai}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Named Entity Recognition}

SpaCy's statistical NER pipeline identifies named spans within article text and
assigns them to entity types. \texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm} (12\,MB, CPU-only)
achieves an F1 of approximately 0.85 on OntoNotes~\cite{spacy2}. Processing is
limited to the first 5\,000 characters per article to bound runtime; the title
and standfirst, which are most informationally dense, always fall within this
window. Entity extraction serves two purposes: (i) it provides structured
metadata for the threat report, identifying which organisations and countries
are implicated in each item; and (ii) it contributes a risk signal, since
articles naming more distinct actors tend to describe more concrete, attributable
threats.

\subsection{Threat Classification}
\label{subsec:cls}

The classifier operates in two stages.

\textbf{Stage 1: Keyword classifier (all 1,228 articles).}
For each article, the normalised keyword density $K_c$ for category $c$ is
\begin{equation}
  K_c = \frac{\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}_c} \mathrm{count}(k,\,\hat{t})}{|\hat{t}|}
  \label{eq:keyword}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{K}_c$ is the keyword set for category $c$, $\hat{t}$ is the
lowercased search text, and $|\hat{t}|$ denotes word count. The article is
assigned to $\arg\max_c K_c$. A normalised score
$S_{\mathrm{kw}} = \min(K_{c^*}/0.05,\;1.0)$ clips at a reference density of
0.05 hits per word.

\textbf{Stage 2: Zero-shot NLI classifier (top-50 by keyword score).}
\texttt{cross-encoder/nli-MiniLM2-L6-H768}~\cite{reimers2019sentencebert} is
applied in zero-shot classification mode~\cite{yin2019benchmark}. The model
scores each of the five category labels as a natural language hypothesis against
the article text, returning a confidence distribution. This stage runs on the
top-50 articles only, bounding prototype runtime at approximately two seconds
per article on a CPU. The highest-confidence label and its score are stored
alongside the keyword result, providing a consistency check and an example of
how a learned model can complement rule-based classification.

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis}

VADER~\cite{hutto2014vader} produces a compound score $v \in [-1,\,1]$ for
the article headline and standfirst. A sentiment risk component is defined as
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{sent}} = \frac{1 - v}{2}
  \label{eq:sentiment}
\end{equation}
so that highly negative text ($v \approx -1$) yields $S_{\mathrm{sent}} \approx 1$
and neutral-to-positive text yields $S_{\mathrm{sent}} \approx 0$. VADER was
chosen over a transformer-based sentiment model because it runs in milliseconds
on any hardware, requires no additional model download, and produces scores that
are directly interpretable by practitioners.

\section{Risk Assessment Framework}
\label{sec:risk}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Component Scores}

Four normalised scores, each in $[0,\,1]$, contribute to the composite:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Keyword score} $S_{\mathrm{kw}}$: normalised keyword density
        (Eq.~\ref{eq:keyword}).
  \item \textbf{Sentiment score} $S_{\mathrm{sent}}$: VADER negativity
        (Eq.~\ref{eq:sentiment}).
  \item \textbf{Recency score} $S_{\mathrm{rec}}$: exponential decay from
        publication date with a 30-day half-life,
        \begin{equation}
          S_{\mathrm{rec}} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\ln 2 \cdot \Delta t}{30}\right),
          \label{eq:recency}
        \end{equation}
        where $\Delta t$ is the article age in days.
  \item \textbf{Entity score} $S_{\mathrm{ent}}$: normalised named entity
        count, $\min(n_e / n_{\max},\;1.0)$, where $n_e$ is the article's
        entity count and $n_{\max}$ is the corpus maximum.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Composite Risk Score}

The final risk score is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Risk} = \Big(0.35\cdot S_{\mathrm{kw}}
    + 0.25\cdot S_{\mathrm{sent}}
    + 0.20\cdot S_{\mathrm{rec}}
    + 0.20\cdot S_{\mathrm{ent}}\Big)
    \times M_c \times 10,
  \label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}
where $M_c$ is a category-specific severity multiplier: Terrorism ($1.5$),
Critical Infrastructure ($1.4$), Cyber ($1.3$), Geopolitical ($1.2$),
Disinformation ($1.0$), and Unknown ($0.8$). These multipliers encode domain
knowledge about the typical operational impact of each category; they are a
deliberate design choice, not a learned parameter. The score is clipped to
$[0,\,10]$ and mapped to four severity labels (Table~\ref{tab:severity}).

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Severity labels and suggested analyst actions}
\label{tab:severity}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Label} & \textbf{Score range} & \textbf{Suggested action} \\
\midrule
Low      & $[0,\,3)$  & Log; review weekly \\
Medium   & $[3,\,6)$  & Monitor; alert on escalation \\
High     & $[6,\,8)$  & Prioritise; analyst review within 24 h \\
Critical & $[8,\,10]$ & Immediate escalation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Threat Report Output}

Two artefacts are produced: \texttt{threat\_report.csv} lists all articles
sorted by descending risk score, with category, severity label, key entities,
and URL; \texttt{threat\_report.json} provides the same data in a format
suitable for ingestion by downstream dashboards or SIEM platforms.
A structured set of per-category \emph{intelligence briefs} additionally
extracts the two highest-scoring articles per threat type, summarising title,
score, date, key organisations, and locations.

\section{Prototype Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Corpus Overview}

After collection and preprocessing, the corpus contains 1,228 unique articles.
Fig.~\ref{fig:catdist} shows the per-category distribution. The dominance of
Terrorism partly reflects the single query term used for that category
(\emph{terrorism}) matching a large volume of recent Guardian reporting, and
the category multiplier ($M_c = 1.5$) subsequently amplifying scores for
high-keyword-density matches.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{threat_category_dist.png}
  \caption{Distribution of 1,228 articles across the five threat categories
  plus Unknown, from keyword classification.}
  \label{fig:catdist}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Risk Score Distribution}

The composite score distribution is right-skewed: 60.8\% of articles fall in
the Medium range ($[3,\,6)$), with 23.6\% High, 5.1\% Critical, and 10.4\%
Low. The corpus mean is 5.09 out of 10. Fig.~\ref{fig:scoredist} shows the
histogram. The highest-scoring articles (scores of 10.0) cover the Golders
Green terrorist attack and its immediate aftermath in the UK, which combine
high keyword density, strong sentiment negativity, and short time since
publication; this behaviour is exactly what the scoring formula is designed
to produce.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{risk_score_dist.png}
  \caption{Risk score distribution across the corpus. The distribution is
  right-skewed with a mean of 5.09 and a tail of Critical items (score $\geq8$).}
  \label{fig:scoredist}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Classifier Comparison}

On the top-50 articles by keyword score, the keyword and NLI classifiers agree
approximately 75\% of the time. Disagreements arise most often when an article
has moderate cyber-related keyword density but the zero-shot model correctly
identifies geopolitical framing (e.g., state-sponsored cyberattack reporting).
These cases confirm that the two stages are complementary: the keyword classifier
provides fast, fully deterministic triage, while the NLI stage captures semantic
context that keyword density alone misses. It is also worth noting a known
limitation: articles covering the Ukraine conflict are occasionally scored as
high-risk Terrorism due to dense use of attack-related vocabulary; finer
subcategory distinctions would reduce this artefact in a production system.

\subsection{Risk versus Sentiment}

Fig.~\ref{fig:scatter} shows the relationship between composite risk score and
VADER sentiment negativity across the corpus. High-risk articles cluster toward
high negativity, but the spread is substantial, confirming that sentiment is a
meaningful but non-dominant signal. Recency and keyword density introduce
the spread, which is the intended behaviour of the composite formula.

\section{Key Considerations}
\label{sec:considerations}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Data Quality and Limitations}

The prototype relies on a single English-language outlet. The Guardian's
editorial decisions determine what is published; emerging threats first
reported in non-English sources or specialist technical forums (threat
intelligence blogs, vulnerability disclosure lists, dark-web forums) are
invisible to the system. A robust production pipeline should integrate
multiple independent sources, including CISA advisories and CVE
feeds~\cite{cvss2019}, and apply cross-lingual NLP.

Keyword classifiers are inherently vocabulary-bound: novel terminology (a
newly coined attack name or a zero-day before public disclosure) will not
match until the taxonomy is updated. Zero-shot NLI partially mitigates this
but is limited by its own training distribution. The scoring weights are
hand-tuned rather than data-driven; without labelled ground truth, there is
no objective basis for claiming they are optimal, and different weighting
configurations would yield different rankings.

\subsection{Ethical and Legal Considerations}

All data is retrieved under The Guardian's developer API terms, which permit
non-commercial use. No personal data beyond article bylines is processed.
However, automated threat labelling at scale carries the risk of generating
false positives that, if acted upon without human review, could cause harm
to individuals or organisations. A human-in-the-loop review step is essential
before any score-driven operational action is taken. The system must not be
applied to monitoring individuals, and any production deployment should
include bias audits to ensure no particular group is systematically
over-represented in high-severity labels.

\subsection{Scalability and Deployment}

The prototype runs on a standard laptop with no GPU. For production deployment,
three architectural changes are required: (i) a scheduled ingestion pipeline
(e.g., Apache Airflow) replacing the manual notebook execution; (ii) a vector
database (e.g., Weaviate or Pinecone) replacing CSV files, enabling semantic
search and similarity-based deduplication across a growing corpus; and
(iii) a fine-tuned cybersecurity NER and classification model replacing the
general-purpose small models. Table~\ref{tab:scalability} summarises these
transitions.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Scalability roadmap: prototype versus production}
\label{tab:scalability}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Prototype} & \textbf{Production} \\
\midrule
Ingestion     & Manual notebook run      & Airflow DAG, multi-source \\
Storage       & JSON + CSV files         & Vector DB (Weaviate) \\
NER model     & spaCy \texttt{sm} (12 MB) & Fine-tuned cybersec NER \\
Classifier    & Keyword + ZS-NLI sample  & Fine-tuned transformer \\
Risk scoring  & Hand-tuned weights       & Active-learning feedback \\
Output        & Static CSV/JSON          & Live Streamlit dashboard \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

A natural long-term evolution is toward \emph{agentic AI}: autonomous agents
that continuously monitor multiple sources, escalate newly identified high-risk
items, coordinate analyst workflows, and refine scoring parameters from
feedback. This architecture, incorporating agent-based reasoning and
multi-source coverage across surface, deep, and open-web channels, represents
the production vision that this prototype is designed to motivate and de-risk.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This paper presented a prototype OSINT threat monitoring system demonstrating
an end-to-end pipeline from API ingestion to ranked threat reports, using only
CPU-friendly, freely available NLP tools. Over 1,228 articles, the pipeline
achieves a corpus mean risk score of 5.09, correctly surfaces Critical events
with scores up to 10.0, and produces structured intelligence briefs across
five threat categories. The two-stage classifier requires no labelled data, and
the composite risk score is fully interpretable. Key limitations, including
single-source coverage and hand-tuned weights, are well-understood starting
points for a production system.

Future work falls into five directions: (i) integrating CISA advisories and CVE
feeds for enriched technical context; (ii) mapping extracted entities to MITRE
ATT\&CK techniques~\cite{strom2018mitre} for structured threat attribution;
(iii) building a knowledge graph of threat actors, tools, and campaigns;
(iv) applying active learning with analyst feedback to refine classification
and scoring weights; and (v) developing a real-time Streamlit dashboard as
the operational interface, evolving over time toward a fully agentic
architecture capable of autonomous multi-source OSINT collection and triage.
